About
Dr. Mahmoud A. Shawky received his B.Sc. (Hons.) from the faculty of Engineering, Alexandria University and his M.Sc. in Authentication Mechanisms in Computer Network Protocols. He earned his Ph.D. in Electronics and Electrical Engineering from the University of Glasgow, where he previously served as a Teaching and Research Assistant and currently works as a lecturer at the German International University. His research interests include cryptography, digital signatures, physical layer security, wireless authentication, and cybersecurity. Dr. Shawky has authored over 50 peer-reviewed publications in prestigious venues such as Nature Communications Engineering and IEEE Transactions. He has chaired sessions at major IEEE conferences including VTC-Fall 2022 and WCNC 2023 and serves as an executive officer for the IEEE Student Branch at Glasgow. He is an Associate Editor for the ICCK Transactions on Information Security and Cryptography and regularly reviews for top IEEE journals. He is also actively involved in Open-RAN and sustainability projects focused on energy-efficient systems.

Research Keywords: Cryptography, Wireless Authentication, Digital Signatures, Physical Layer Security, Cybersecurity, Open-RAN, Sustainable Communications, Number Theory, Secret Key Generation, Intelligent Reflecting Surfaces

🚀 Exciting News! New Research Publication in 6G Security & Trustworthy AI 🚀
I am thrilled to announce that our paper, “SemantiGuard: AI-Driven Cyber Threat Intelligence for 6G Semantic Communications,” has been accepted for presentation and publication at the IEEE Vehicular Technology Conference (VTC2026-Fall-IEEE Vehicular Technology Society) in Boston, USA!
Our work will be featured in W11: Trustworthy and Privacy-Preserving AI for Secure Vehicular Networks.

This research represents a novel scientific output emerging directly from the Cyber Threat Intelligence course at the Faculty of Informatics and Computer Science, German International University - GIU, Cairo, Egypt. It showcases how advanced academic coursework can translate into cutting-edge, peer-reviewed contributions to international venues.

💡 The Challenge & Our Solution
As 6G networks transition toward Semantic Communication (SemCom), they become vulnerable to low-energy, gradient-optimized Semantic Adversarial Attacks. These stealthy threats hide beneath the thermal noise floor, altering the transmitted meaning while remaining invisible to traditional energy-based security systems.

SemantiGuard addresses this by operating in parallel with the decoding process. Instead of checking signal power, it uses unsupervised geometric anomaly detection (Isolation Forest) to monitor the high-dimensional latent manifold, successfully isolating and rejecting malicious perturbations.

Key Highlights:
- Spatial-Aware Defense: Detects invisible adversarial threats based on their unique geometric positioning within a 4D semantic latent space.
- Proven Resilience: Achieves over 84% detection accuracy even in highly noisy radio environments, distinguishing smart jamming from natural channel noise.
- Vehicular Safety: Provides a robust security layer vital for protecting critical control messages in autonomous and energy-sustainable 6G architectures.

🤝 A Global Collaboration
This milestone is the result of a dedicated international collaboration. I would like to express my sincere gratitude to my co-authors for their invaluable contributions, with special thanks to Dr. Syed Tariq Shah for his exceptional guidance and support throughout this work.

Congratulations to the team:
- Evram Magdy(German International University - GIU): M.Sc. Student & Lead Author
- Dr. Syed Tariq Shah (University of Essex, UK)
- Dr. Hassan Malik (University of East Anglia, UK)
- Dr. Walid M. Saad (The Egyptian Technical Research and Development Center)

Thank you to the Faculty of Informatics and Computer Science, German International University - GIU, Cairo, Egypt for their continued institutional support, and to the workshop organizers for providing a vital platform to address these critical 6G safety challenges.

1mo • Edited • 

Distributed systems theory has been eagerly anticipating the emergence of LLM-agents, and the recent project from the Arab Academy for Science, Technology and Maritime Transport (AASTMT) exemplifies this evolution.

A dedicated team, including myself, raghda ashraf, Ahmed Khaled, and Kareem Hesham, has open-sourced BMAFS, which addresses the hallucinating-LLM problem through the lens of Byzantine fault tolerance. This perspective is crucial, as it reflects the nature of the issue: a single prompt-injected agent can produce plausible yet incorrect outputs while remaining operational, mirroring the failure mode that PBFT was designed to tackle back in 1999.

Our setup features five forensic analyst agents, each leveraging a different model family to minimize correlated failures. We are implementing classical PBFT with n ≥ 3f+1 and a Raft-style leader election. Importantly, our test suite effectively exercises the f-bound; when three out of five agents fail simultaneously, consensus appropriately stalls. A passing test that confirms a failure illustrates the correct experimental design for verifying that a bound is tight.

Repo: https://lnkd.in/d_aeN42P
Supervisor: Prof. Mahmoud Shawky

Two key aspects of this project are noteworthy:
1. The application emphasizes the Byzantine assumption rather than relying on a contrived adversary; the primary challenge arises from the LLM call itself.
2. We incorporate HMAC and explicitly identify EdDSA + post-quantum signatures as future work, rather than implying that the prototype is ready for production.

This project deserves recognition for anyone engaged in multi-agent systems or BFT consensus.

1mo • Edited • 

After weeks of late nights, way too much coffee, and a lot of trial and error… we finally demoed our Distributed Systems project.
Honestly, I’m just proud of what we managed to build.
We created BMAFS — a Byzantine-Robust Multi-Agent LLM Forensic System.
The idea is kind of crazy when you think about it: five AI “analysts” sitting in a virtual SOC, each looking at the same evidence but from a completely different angle — memory, network, files, MITRE ATT&CK mapping, plus a judge.
They don’t trust each other. Any of them could fail, lie, or just stop responding… and the system still has to reach a single, correct verdict.
It’s basically where distributed systems theory meets LLM agents — two worlds that don’t usually overlap much.
Some of the things we built into it:
5 agents running on 4 different model families (on purpose — diversity = resilience)
A simplified PBFT consensus (pre-prepare → prepare → commit) built from scratch
Raft-style leader election — we literally kill the leader mid-demo and it recovers
Replicated state machine with identical logs across all nodes
Byzantine fault injection (liar / silent / equivocator modes)
HMAC-signed messaging over Redis
A live dashboard showing everything happening in real time
Full Docker setup + tests covering safety, liveness, and edge cases
Everything actually works together… which still feels a bit unreal.
The moment I won’t forget:
Watching five different LLMs argue over a Mimikatz sample, while one of them is intentionally lying… and the rest still reach the correct decision and write the exact same result across all replicas.
That was the point where it really clicked.
Big respect to the team:
Ahmed Khaled
Kareem Hesham
raghda ashraf
And huge thanks to prof. Mahmoud Shawky for pushing us to go beyond just “it works” and actually prove the theory behind it.
Definitely one of the toughest (and most rewarding) projects we’ve worked 
on.
Onto the next.
